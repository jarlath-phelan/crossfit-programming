    Nutrition — Fueling the Program¶

    Fueling reference. This is the pillar that feeds the training: it sets a protein target, a way to fuel slow recomposition without obsessing, what to eat around morning sessions (including the espresso and the tendon pre-load), how to recover from a hard or restless night, and a short, honest supplement protocol. It is deliberately non-dogmatic and forkable — a set of defaults and principles, not a meal plan you must obey.

    This doc is forkable and generic. It is written for "the athlete" / "you" so anyone can clone the repo and adapt it. The illustrative case running through it — a masters athlete who is an omnivore with no restrictions, wants protein-first guidance and to eyeball the rest, mixes home cooking with convenience food, drinks socially on weekends, and judges progress by the mirror and performance (not the scale) — is just an example that makes the defaults concrete. Swap in your own profile and the same logic holds.

    Companion docs: science-foundations.md is the why (the evidence behind every number here — this doc cites it heavily); training-system.md is the coach (where these meals land in the day and the week); functional-design.md is the app UX; decisions.md is the log of athlete choices that set these defaults; and testing-protocol.md is the Week 1–2 baseline block. The guardrails the planner obeys live in CLAUDE.md.

    A few ground rules, mirroring science-foundations.md:

    • Honesty about evidence strength is a feature. Each recommendation below is tagged [strong], [moderate], or [suggestive/mechanistic] so a forker can judge how hard to lean on it.
    • Protein-first, eyeball the rest. This whole doc is built so you hit one number reliably (protein) and steer everything else by simple defaults and the mirror — no macro logging required.
    • The scale is not the target. Recomposition (lose fat / gain muscle at once) routinely shows up in the mirror and in performance long before — or instead of — a moving bodyweight. We track those.

    1. The one number that matters: protein¶

    If you change nothing else, change this. Protein is the single highest-leverage nutrition variable for someone building upper-body muscle, recomposing, and recovering from hard training.

    Target: ~1.6–2.2 g of protein per kg of bodyweight per day. [strong]

    • The classic meta-analysis places the point of diminishing returns for muscle gain at ~1.6 g/kg/day, with a safety ceiling around 2.2 g/kg/day to cover individual variation (Morton et al., Br J Sports Med, 2018). Going higher is not harmful but buys little extra muscle.
    • In a caloric deficit (slow recomp) the right move is the upper end — ~1.8–2.2 g/kg — because higher protein spares lean mass while you lose fat (Helms et al., 2014; Longland et al., 2016).
    • For a masters athlete, this matters more, not less — though less than the scare stories imply. Trained over-35 athletes show protein metabolism "indistinguishable from athletes half their age" (Moore et al., 2021; see science §5), so the total doesn't balloon with age — but hitting it consistently and evenly does the work.

    Worked example (so the math is concrete). A ~95 kg athlete: 1.6 × 95 ≈ 150 g/day floor, 2.2 × 95 ≈ 210 g/day ceiling. A simple target band of ~165–200 g/day sits comfortably in the recomp-friendly upper half. Substitute your own bodyweight; the band scales linearly.

    How to hit it without logging¶

    You do not need to weigh food or track macros. Use the palm method plus a per-meal floor:

    • ~0.3–0.4 g/kg protein per meal, which for a ~95 kg athlete is ~30–40 g — roughly one to two palm-sized portions of a protein food per meal. [moderate] Per-meal dosing of ~0.4 g/kg maximizes the muscle-protein-synthesis response (Schoenfeld & Aragon, 2018).
    • Anchor every meal and snack with a protein first, then build carbs/veg/fat around it by eye. If protein leads, the "eyeball the rest" approach lands in a good place by default.
    • 3–5 protein hits a day (meals + a snack or two) spreads the dose. Even distribution beats skewing it all to dinner. [moderate]
    • Rule of thumb to "see" 30–40 g: a chicken breast, a can of tuna, 3 large eggs plus a bit more, a ~150 g tub of Greek yogurt + a scoop of whey, 200 g lean mince, or one scoop of whey (~25 g) topped up with milk or yogurt.

    A protein-first day on autopilot (no weighing):

    Breakfast   eggs + Greek yogurt, or yogurt + whey + berries        ~35–45 g
Lunch       palm-or-two of chicken/beef/fish/tofu + carbs + veg    ~40–50 g
Snack       whey shake, or cottage cheese, or jerky + milk         ~25–35 g
Dinner      palm-or-two of a protein + carbs + veg                 ~45–55 g
(optional)  casein-ish before bed: yogurt / cottage cheese / milk  ~20–30 g
                                                          total ≈ 165–210 g

    What this means for the program. Protein is the only thing worth counting, and even that you can count on your fingers. Lead every meal with a protein, aim for the upper half of the range while recomping, and you've handled ~80% of the nutrition outcome.

    2. Total energy: steering a slow recomp without the scale¶

    The goal is slow body recomposition — lose fat, build the chest/back/delt/core physique — judged by the mirror and performance, not bodyweight. That changes how we steer energy.

    Why ignore the scale here. [strong, in context] When you simultaneously add muscle and lose fat, the scale can sit flat, drift up, or drift down — all while you visibly improve. Recomposition is well-documented in trained-but-detrained athletes returning to training, exactly this athlete's situation (re-onboarding after a gap). The scale measures the sum of two changes moving in opposite directions, so it's the wrong instrument. We track the mirror, the tape (waist), and the training-log instead.

    The energy setting for slow recomp: a small deficit or maintenance, protein held high. [moderate]

    • A mild deficit (~10–20% below maintenance, roughly 200–500 kcal) with high protein and hard training is the best-evidenced recomp setup — fat goes down, lean mass holds or climbs (Longland 2016; Barakat et al. recomposition review, 2020). Aggressive deficits cost muscle and wreck training quality; this is a gentle lever, in keeping with the program's longevity-over-speed ethos.
    • You don't have to count calories to run this. Protein-first eating is intrinsically satiating and tends to auto-regulate intake downward. Steer by outcomes, not arithmetic (below).

    The no-logging recomp dashboard¶

    Check these on a slow cadence; adjust food by a notch, not a spreadsheet:

    Signal Cadence If trending wrong Adjustment
    Mirror (same light, fasted AM) every ~2–4 wks softer, not leaner trim a notch of carbs/fat at the meals furthest from training
    Waist / belt notch every ~2–4 wks waist up small deficit nudge; protein stays fixed
    Training performance / energy (the log) continuous strength or MetCon output sliding, sessions feel flat you've cut too hard — add food back; performance wins
    Recovery & sleep (readiness trend) continuous readiness dropping, hungry/cold/cranky ease the deficit; recomp is a marathon

    The tie-breaker rule: if the mirror and the training log ever disagree, the training log wins. A leaner reflection is not worth a sliding deadlift or a flat Fran. Eat to train; lean comes as a by-product of consistency, not starvation.

    What this means for the program. Set a gentle energy deficit (or just maintenance with high protein) and judge it by mirror + waist + performance on a 2–4 week cadence — never by daily bodyweight. Carbs and fats are the adjustment dial; protein is fixed. When in doubt, eat slightly more and let training quality lead. This is recomposition by patience, matching the tendons-first / no-rush philosophy in training-system §1.

    3. Fueling around morning training¶

    The program trains in the morning (see science §1). Three fueling questions come up: caffeine, the tendon pre-load, and whether to eat before training.

    3.1 The espresso (caffeine timing)¶

    Caffeine is the program's strategic morning lever — it counteracts the early-day power dip (science §1). The fueling rules:

    • Dose & timing: ~3 mg/kg (~285 mg for a ~95 kg athlete) taken ~45–60 min pre-session. [strong] That's roughly 2–3 espressos depending on the machine. The performance window peaks ~45–60 min after intake.
    • Reserve it for the hardest sessions — heavy strict strength, skill days, sprinty conditioning — and cycle it rather than dosing every day. Habituation blunts the effect; cycling keeps it potent.
    • AM-only, and titrate down if sleep suffers. [strong] This athlete's main recovery limiter is sleep quality, not duration (decisions), and caffeine has a ~5–6 h half-life — an afternoon dose still has a quarter of it onboard near midnight. Keep it before ~noon; if sleep gets restless, cut the dose before you cut anything else. The full traffic-light gating lives in training-system §4 (caffeine timing).
    • A small caffeine dose is fine on a near-empty stomach for most people; if it bothers your gut, take it with the pre-session collagen + a few carbs (below).

    3.2 The tendon pre-load: collagen + vitamin C¶

    This is a small, cheap, on-target intervention for the program's central injury risk — connective tissue (science §5).

    • 15 g hydrolyzed collagen (or gelatin) + ~50 mg vitamin C, taken ~60 min before tendon-loading work. [moderate] Taken an hour pre-load, this roughly doubled a collagen-synthesis marker (Shaw/Baar et al., 2017), and a 2024 RCT found hydrolyzed collagen + resistance training improved patellar-tendon adaptation in middle-aged men. The ~1 h timing matters: the amino acids need to be circulating when the tissue is loaded.
    • Vitamin C is the cofactor for collagen cross-linking — a glass of orange juice (~50 mg) covers it, or a tablet. No need to overdo it.
    • When: the gymnastics / strict-strength / skill days — exactly the sessions adding new upper-body pressing, pulling, dip and false-grip load, where tendon outpaces are the risk. On pure conditioning or wellness days it's optional.
    • Honest evidence note: the collagen-synthesis-marker data are robust; long-term injury-prevention RCTs are still thin. Treat this as low-risk and biologically well-supported, not proven to prevent injury. It's cheap insurance on the program's known weak link, not a magic shield.

    Practical stack: the pre-session collagen + vitamin C can ride along with the morning espresso ~45–60 min out — one small ritual covers both the caffeine window and the tendon window.

    3.3 Eat before, or train fasted?¶

    • For a ~45–60 min session, fasted is fine if you prefer it — performance over short, mostly strength/skill sessions isn't meaningfully impaired by an overnight fast for most people. [moderate]
    • If a session is long, sprinty, or feels flat fasted, a small carb + protein hit ~30–60 min before helps (e.g. a banana + the whey/collagen, or toast + eggs). Carbs matter more the more glycolytic/MetCon-heavy the day. [moderate]
    • The pre-session collagen counts as a tiny feed — a few grams of protein on board pre-training is mildly anabolic and settles the stomach with caffeine.
    • Don't force a big breakfast before heavy AM work if it sits badly. The priority is the post-session feed (next section); the pre-feed is optional and individual.

    What this means for the program. On hard GREEN morning days: ~2–3 espressos (~3 mg/kg) and 15 g collagen + vitamin C, ~45–60 min before training; optional light carb+protein if you train better fed. AM-only caffeine, cycled, dialed down before it touches sleep. On easy/wellness/RED days, skip the caffeine and the pre-load — they're tools for the sessions that need them.

    4. Post-session recovery nutrition¶

    Recovery nutrition matters more here than for a typical lifter, for two athlete-specific reasons: this athlete gets very sore and sleeps restlessly, so the levers that blunt soreness and support sleep earn their place.

    • Protein within a few hours, not a frantic 30-min "window." [strong] The "anabolic window" is far wider than gym folklore claims — total daily protein and per-meal dosing matter more than racing a shake (Schoenfeld/Aragon). A normal protein-led meal within ~1–2 h of training is plenty. If the next meal is hours off, a whey shake (~25–40 g) post-session is a convenient way to land a dose.
    • Carbs to refill and to aid recovery. [moderate] After glycolytic MetCons or sprint work, carbs replenish glycogen and, paired with protein, support recovery. You don't need to count them — put a real carb (rice, potato, oats, fruit, bread) on the post-training plate. Bias carbs toward the training meals; that's also the natural place to trim them on lower-output days (see §2).
    • Soreness (DOMS): manage expectations honestly. [strong] No food erases soreness. But adequate protein + total energy + carbs supports the repair process, and a few specifics have modest support:
    • Tart cherry juice has the best DOMS/recovery evidence of the food options — modest reductions in soreness and markers after damaging work (Bell et al. reviews). [moderate] Optional, situational — e.g. after a deliberately hard or novel session.
    • Omega-3s (oily fish / supplement) may modestly reduce soreness and support recovery over time. [suggestive] (See §5.)
    • Stay fed and hydrated. Under-eating amplifies soreness and slows repair more than any "recovery food" fixes it.
    • The sleep-quality lever is partly a nutrition lever. [moderate] Because restless sleep is the #1 recovery limiter here:
    • Protein before bed (casein-like: Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, milk, or a casein shake, ~20–40 g) supplies overnight amino acids and doesn't hurt sleep. [moderate]
    • Keep the evening meal from being too late, too large, or too boozy — all degrade sleep quality.
    • Caffeine discipline (§3.1) is the biggest dietary sleep lever — re-emphasized because it's that important here.
    • Pair this with the program's evening paced-breathing block, the primary non-food sleep tool (science §2).

    What this means for the program. After training, eat a normal protein-led meal with real carbs within a couple of hours (a whey shake bridges the gap if the meal is far off). Don't chase a magic window or a magic soreness food — keep total protein and energy adequate, hydrate, and let sleep do the heavy lifting. A pre-bed casein-type protein and strict caffeine timing quietly support the restless-sleep limiter.

    5. The weekend-alcohol → Monday-recovery note¶

    Alcohol is social, mostly weekends here, and that's a legitimate choice — this program optimizes for a sustainable, enjoyable life, not monastic compliance. The job of this section is honesty about the cost and a practical way to spend around it.

    What the evidence says, plainly. [moderate–strong]

    • Alcohol degrades sleep architecture — it can shorten sleep latency but suppresses REM and fragments the back half of the night. For someone whose limiter is sleep quality, this is the main cost. A boozy Saturday night shows up as a flat, low-readiness Sunday/Monday.
    • Alcohol blunts muscle protein synthesis acutely and can impair recovery and next-day performance, especially with larger doses post-training (Parr et al., 2014). [moderate]
    • It dehydrates and adds "empty" energy — relevant to a recomp, though the bigger lever is sleep and recovery, not the calories per se.

    This is not "don't drink." It's "spend it where it costs least, and let the system absorb it."

    • Don't pair heavy drinking with the day's hardest training — especially not right after a muscle-damaging session.
    • Hydrate and get a protein-led meal in around drinking; it won't undo the sleep hit but helps the rest.
    • Expect Monday to read AMBER, and that's fine — the readiness engine is designed for this. A low-readiness Monday after a social weekend should pull the day toward skill/strength + mobility/wellness, not a crushing MetCon. The system gates this automatically (training-system §5 / science §3).
    • Structurally, this is why the week undulates. Putting a hard MetCon on Monday is the wrong default for a weekend-social athlete; the planner biases Monday toward lower-CNS, recovery-friendly work and lets the harder days land mid-week when readiness has rebounded.

    What this means for the program. Weekend social drinking stays — it's part of a sustainable life. The program plans around it: don't stack booze on the hardest sessions, hydrate and eat protein, and let Monday autoregulate down. The cost is mostly sleep quality, so the same evening-breathing and caffeine-timing levers that protect sleep are the ones that buffer the weekend.

    6. Supplement protocol¶

    A short, evidence-tiered stack — the few things worth taking, and honestly what each does. The athlete currently takes none and is open to suggestions; this is the recommended starting set, ranked by how well it's supported. Nothing here is required to make the program work; supplements are the last ~5%, after protein, energy, sleep, and training consistency.

    Supplement Dose What it's for Evidence
    Creatine monohydrate 5 g/day, every day, any time Strength, power, lean mass, work capacity; some cognitive/recovery upside. Cheap, deeply studied. [strong] — one of the most robustly supported ergogenic aids in existence. No loading phase needed; 5 g/day saturates in ~3–4 weeks.
    Whey protein 1 scoop (~25 g) as needed to hit the daily target A fast, convenient way to land the protein number — not magic, just efficient protein. [strong] — for total protein adequacy. Whole-food protein is equivalent; whey just makes the target easier on busy days.
    Collagen + vitamin C 15 g collagen + ~50 mg C, ~60 min pre tendon work Tendon/connective-tissue support — the program's central injury lever (§3.2). [moderate] — strong on collagen-synthesis markers; long-term injury-prevention RCTs still thin. Low-risk, on-target.
    Vitamin D₃ ~1000–2000 IU/day (more if blood-tested low) Bone, muscle function, immune, mood/sleep support — corrects a deficiency that's common, especially in winter / less sun. [moderate] — clear benefit if you're low (worth a blood test), little extra if you're already replete.
    Omega-3 (EPA/DHA) ~1–2 g combined EPA+DHA/day (or oily fish 2–3×/wk) Recovery, soreness, anti-inflammatory support; may help sleep. [suggestive–moderate] — real but modest effects; food sources count.

    Notes & honesty:

    • Creatine and whey are the two that earn their place unconditionally. If the stack got cut to two, it's these.
    • Creatine causes water retention inside the muscle — a good sign (cell volumization), but it can nudge the scale up a pound or two in the first weeks. Since we ignore the scale anyway (§2), this is a non-issue here — just don't let it spook you.
    • Whey isn't special beyond convenience — it's protein. Skip it if you reliably hit the target with food. It exists in this stack because the athlete is short on time and mixes cooking with convenience.
    • Vitamin D is a "test, don't guess" item — dose to your actual blood level rather than guessing.
    • Skip the rest. BCAAs (redundant if total protein is adequate), fat-burners, most pre-workouts (caffeine already covered), testosterone boosters — not worth it for this program's goals. Caffeine is covered by the espresso (§3.1), so a separate pre-workout is redundant.
    • This is general guidance, not medical advice. Anyone on medication or with a health condition should run a stack past their doctor; the vitamin D blood test is a genuinely worthwhile check.

    What this means for the program. Daily creatine 5 g + whey to fill the protein gap are the core. Collagen + vitamin C pre-loads tendon days (§3.2). Vitamin D (dose to bloodwork) and omega-3 round out recovery/sleep support. Everything else is noise for these goals.

    7. Fast, high-protein options (for someone short on time)¶

    The athlete mixes cooking and convenience and is often time-pressed, so the default toolkit biases to fast. Each lands ~25–45 g protein with minimal effort. Mix, match, repeat — variety is nice but hitting the number is the point.

    No-cook / grab-and-go (~25–40 g): - Greek yogurt (large tub) + whey scoop + berries + honey - Cottage cheese + fruit, or + tomatoes & black pepper (savory) - Whey shake in milk (~35 g) — the fastest 30 s of protein there is - Two cans of tuna or a tin of sardines + crackers / on toast - Beef/biltong jerky + a glass of milk or a shake - Pre-cooked chicken/deli meat roll-ups + cheese - Protein bar (check it's actually ~20 g+ protein, not a candy bar) + milk - A handful of edamame + a yogurt

    5–10 minute cook (~30–50 g): - 3–4 egg scramble/omelet + cheese (+ leftover veg/meat thrown in) - Microwave-rice pouch + pre-cooked chicken/prawns + soy/sriracha - Tinned beans or lentils + tinned tuna/eggs on toast - Protein oats: oats + milk + whey + peanut butter (overnight or microwave) - High-protein pasta (lentil/chickpea) + a quick sauce + parmesan - Smoked salmon + eggs + bagel

    Batch-once, eat-fast (cook on a quieter day, reheat all week): - A tray of chicken thighs / lean mince / pulled pork → into rice bowls, wraps, salads all week - Hard-boil a half-dozen eggs → instant protein snacks - A big pot of chili or a curry with beans + meat → reheats in minutes, scales protein easily

    Eating-out / takeaway defaults (convenience without losing the plot): - Lead with the protein: grilled chicken/steak/fish, burrito bowls (double meat), poke, kebab meat + salad, sushi with extra edamame, burger (even bun-and-all) over fries-as-the-meal. - "Protein first, eyeball the rest" works at a restaurant too — pick the dish around its protein and the rest sorts itself.

    What this means for the program. Keep 3–4 of these on permanent autopilot so a busy day still hits ~165–200 g without thought. Convenience is not the enemy of the goal — missing the protein number is. A whey shake and a yogurt are a perfectly legitimate "meal" on a wrecked day.

    8. The whole thing, in one paragraph¶

    Eat protein first and hit ~1.6–2.2 g/kg/day (~165–200 g for a ~95 kg athlete) by leading every meal with a palm-or-two of a protein food — that one habit is ~80% of the result, and you can count it on your fingers. Eyeball the rest, steering a gentle slow-recomp by the mirror, the waist, and the training log — never the scale (carbs/fats are the dial; if performance ever slips, eat more). On hard morning days, take ~3 mg/kg caffeine (a couple of espressos) and 15 g collagen + vitamin C ~45–60 min pre, keep caffeine AM-only and cycled so it never touches your restless sleep, then recover with a protein-led meal + real carbs and a pre-bed casein-type protein. Weekend drinking stays — just don't stack it on the hardest session, and let Monday autoregulate down. Take creatine 5 g daily and whey to fill gaps (the two that always earn their place), plus collagen+C, vitamin D, and omega-3 for tendons and recovery. Everything else is noise. This is fueling for longevity and consistency, matching the rest of the program — non-dogmatic, sustainable, and built to be forked.
